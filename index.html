PART I: FOUNDATIONS

UNIT 1: THE FRENCH ALPHABET & PRONUNCIATION

1.1 The French Alphabet

The French alphabet has 26 letters, just like English. However, the pronunciation is quite different. Here's how to pronounce each letter:

Letter French Pronunciation Letter French Pronunciation
A ah N en
B bey O oh
C say P pey
D day Q koo
E uh R air
F eff S ess
G zhay T tey
H ash U oo
I ee V vey
J zhee W doubluh-vay
K kah X eeks
L el Y ee-grec
M em Z zed

Canadian French Note (🍁): Quebec French pronunciation of letters is similar to standard French, though Quebecers may pronounce the letter "R" with a slight guttural quality, keeping older French vowel sounds that have changed in France.

1.2 French Vowel Sounds

French vowels can be pronounced differently depending on accents and context. Here are the main vowel sounds:

Sound Pronunciation Examples
a, à, â "ah" (father) ma (my), pâte (dough)
e, é "ay" (café) été (summer), cher (dear)
è, ê "eh" (bed) crème (cream), tête (head)
i, î "ee" (see) signe (sign), midi (noon)
o, ô "oh" (go) rose (rose), côte (coast)
u, û "oo" (moon) tu (you), jûs (juice)
y "ee" yo-yo, type

Nasal Vowels (Important!): When a vowel is followed by M or N at the end of a word or before another consonant, it becomes "nasalized" (pronounced through the nose):

Nasal Sound Examples
an, en "ahn" – plan (plan), dent (tooth)
on, om "ohn" – bon (good), nombre (number)
in, im "ahn" – fin (end), simple (simple)
un, um "ahn" – un (a), parfum (perfume)

Canadian French Note (🍁): Quebec French speakers maintain stronger nasal vowels than France French speakers, with more resonance and fronted nasal sounds (an, on, in). These differences make Québécois sound more distinct.

1.3 Consonant Sounds

Most French consonants sound similar to English, but some are different:

Consonant Pronunciation Examples
C (before a, o, u) "k" sound café (café), cas (case)
C (before e, i) "s" sound cerise (cherry), cité (city)
G (before a, o, u) "g" sound gare (station), gout (taste)
G (before e, i) "zh" sound rouge (red), girafe (giraffe)
H silent hôtel (hotel), homme (man)
J "zh" sound jeu (game), jamais (never)
R guttural (throat) rue (street), rouge (red)
S "z" (between vowels) maison (house)
S "s" (at start or end) salut (hello), pas (not)
W "v" sound wagon (wagon)
X "ks" or "gz" six (six), examen (exam)
Z "z" sound zéro (zero), zone (zone)

Canadian French Note (🍁): Quebec French features "affrication" in informal speech—the sounds "t" and "d" before "i" and "e" become "ts" and "dz" respectively. For example, "ti" becomes "tsi" and "di" becomes "dzi" in fast speech.

1.4 Accents & Special Marks

French uses five accent marks that change pronunciation:

Mark Name Purpose Examples
´ Acute accent Indicates open pronunciation café (café), école (school)
` Grave accent Affects vowel sound où (where), très (very)
^ Circumflex Often changes vowel sound tête (head), château (castle)
¨ Diaeresis Separates vowel sounds naïve (naive), Noël (Christmas)
¢ Cedilla Softens the C ça (that), français (French)

1.5 Liaison & Elision

Liaison occurs when a final silent consonant is pronounced before a word beginning with a vowel:

  • les enfants – The "s" in "les" is pronounced: "ley-zahn-fahn"
  • un ami – The "n" in "un" is pronounced: "uh-nah-mee"
  • vous êtes – The "s" in "vous" is pronounced: "voo-zet"

Elision occurs when a vowel is dropped before a word beginning with a vowel:

  • l'ami (the friend) – Instead of "le ami"
  • l'école (the school) – Instead of "la école"
  • j'ai (I have) – Instead of "je ai"

1.6 Pronunciation Practice

Try pronouncing these common French words:

  1. Bonjour (hello) – bohn-zhoor
  2. Merci (thank you) – mare-see
  3. S'il vous plaît (please) – see-voo-pleh
  4. Au revoir (goodbye) – oh-ruh-vwahr
  5. Excusez-moi (excuse me) – ex-koo-zay-mwah
  6. Ça va? (How are you?) – sah-vah
  7. Je m'appelle (My name is) – zhuh-mah-pell
  8. Enchanté (pleased to meet you) – ohn-shahn-tay
  9. Oui (yes) – wee
  10. Non (no) – nohn

Canadian French Note (🍁): When you hear Quebec French, you'll notice the characteristic "chantant" (melodic) quality. The stress often falls on the last syllable of words or phrases, unlike France French where stress is more evenly distributed. Listen carefully to how Quebecers extend vowels for emphasis.

UNIT 2: ESSENTIAL GREETINGS & INTRODUCTIONS

2.1 Formal Greetings

French has formal and informal ways to greet people. Use formal greetings with people you don't know, older people, or in professional settings.

Greeting Expressions:

French English Pronunciation
Bonjour Hello / Good day bohn-zhoor
Bonsoir Good evening bohn-swahr
Bonne nuit Good night bun-nwee
Bon matin Good morning bohn-mah-tahn

Appropriate Times:

  • Bonjour – Used all day until evening falls (typically before 6 PM)
  • Bonsoir – Used after 6 PM
  • Bonne nuit – Said only when going to bed
  • Bon matin – Less common, mainly in Canada

Response Formulas:

When someone says "Bonjour" to you, you should always respond with "Bonjour" back. It's considered very rude not to!

Examples:

  • Person A: "Bonjour, comment allez-vous?" (Hello, how are you?)
  • Person B: "Bonjour, ça va bien, merci." (Hello, I'm doing well, thank you.)

Asking:

2.7 Canadian French Greeting Differences (🍁)

In Quebec, there are some unique greeting customs and expressions:

  1. "Allô" – This is very common for phone greetings. In France, they say "Allô" less frequently.

  2. "Ça va?" with "moé" and "toé" – In casual Québécois speech, you'll hear "Ça va, moé?" instead of "Ça va, moi?" This is very informal and represents old French vowel sounds.

  3. "Chu bon" – This informal contraction means "Je suis bon" (I'm good). You'll hear it in casual conversation.

  4. Different stress patterns – Quebec French often stresses the last syllable of a phrase: bon-JOUR instead of BON-jour.

  5. More nasal pronunciation – Quebec greetings sound more nasal than France French, especially in words like "bon," "rien," and "bien."

Example Exchange in Quebec:

  • A: "Ça va?"
  • B: "Chu bien, moé. Pis toé?" (I'm good, me. And you? - very informal)
  • A: "Pas mal!"

UNIT 3: THE VERB "ÊTRE" (TO BE)

3.1 Introduction to "Être"

"Être" (to be) is one of the most important verbs in French. It's irregular, meaning it doesn't follow standard patterns. You must memorize its conjugation.

Infinitive: Être (to be)

3.2 Present Tense Conjugation of "Être"

Here's how "être" changes in the present tense:

Subject French English Pronunciation
I je suis I am zhuh-swee
You (informal) tu es You are too-ay
He / It il est He is eel-ay
She / It elle est She is el-ay
One / We (informal) on est One is / We are ohn-ay
We nous sommes We are noo-som
You (formal/plural) vous êtes You are voo-zet
They (masculine) ils sont They are eel-sohn
They (feminine) elles sont They are el-sohn

Pronoun Conjugation
Je suis
Tu es
Il/Elle/On est
Nous sommes
Vous êtes
Ils/Elles sont

3.3 Using "Être" – Main Functions

1. With Adjectives (describing qualities):

  • "Je suis heureux." (I am happy.)
  • "Elle est intelligente." (She is intelligent.)
  • "Nous sommes fatigués." (We are tired.)
  • "Vous êtes symphatique." (You are nice.)

2. To Identify People (professions, nationality, status):

  • "Je suis professeur." (I am a teacher.)
  • "Il est français." (He is French.)
  • "Elle est médecin." (She is a doctor.)
  • "Nous sommes étudiants." (We are students.)

3. Telling Time and Dates:

  • "Il est 9 heures." (It is 9 o'clock.)
  • "Quel jour sommes-nous?" (What day are we? / What's the date?)
  • "Nous sommes lundi." (It is Monday.)

4. Indicating Location (with "c'est"):

  • "C'est à Paris." (It's in Paris.)
  • "Où es-tu?" (Where are you?)

3.4 Key Expressions with "Être"

French has many fixed expressions using "être" where English uses different verbs:

French English Literal
avoir peur to be afraid to have fear
avoir faim to be hungry to have hunger
avoir soif to be thirsty to have thirst
avoir froid to be cold to have cold
avoir chaud to be hot to have hot
avoir mal to have pain / to hurt to have pain
avoir sommeil to be sleepy to have sleep

Wait! These use "avoir," not "être" – but they're important to know!

3.5 Example Sentences with "Être"

Making Statements:

  1. "Je suis Marc." – I am Marc.
  2. "Tu es très intelligent." – You are very intelligent.
  3. "Il est canadien." – He is Canadian.
  4. "Elle est infirmière." – She is a nurse.
  5. "Nous sommes amis." – We are friends.
  6. "Vous êtes occupés?" – Are you busy?
  7. "Ils sont à l'école." – They are at school.
  8. "Elles sont professeurs." – They are teachers.

Asking Questions with "Être":

  1. "Suis-je correct?" – Am I correct?
  2. "Es-tu heureux?" – Are you happy?
  3. "Est-il ici?" – Is he here?
  4. "Est-elle malade?" – Is she sick?
  5. "Sommes-nous en retard?" – Are we late?
  6. "Êtes-vous prêts?" – Are you ready?
  7. "Sont-ils fatigués?" – Are they tired?

With "Est-ce que" (easier way to ask questions):

  1. "Est-ce que je suis correct?" – Am I correct?
  2. "Est-ce que tu es heureux?" – Are you happy?
  3. "Est-ce qu'il est occupé?" – Is he busy?

3.6 Negative Sentences with "Être"

To make a sentence negative, put "ne" before the verb and "pas" after it:

Structure: Subject + ne + être + pas + adjective/noun

French English
Je ne suis pas médecin. I am not a doctor.
Tu n'es pas occupé. You are not busy.
Il n'est pas français. He is not French.
Elle n'est pas ici. She is not here.
Nous ne sommes pas fatigués. We are not tired.
Vous n'êtes pas heureux. You are not happy.
Ils ne sont pas malades. They are not sick.
Elles ne sont pas tard. They are not late.

Note: When "ne" comes before a vowel, it becomes "n'" (n'es, n'est, n'êtes).

3.7 Canadian French Notes for "Être" (🍁)

  1. "Chu" for "je suis" – In very informal Quebec speech, you might hear "chu occupé" instead of "je suis occupé" (I'm busy). This is slang and not acceptable in formal writing or speaking.

  2. Pronunciation of "est" – Quebec speakers often pronounce "est" as "é" quickly, almost like "c'é" instead of "c'est."

  3. Stress patterns – The stress in Quebec French falls more on the final syllable: "je-SUIS" rather than "JE-suis."

UNIT 4: THE VERB "AVOIR" (TO HAVE)

4.1 Introduction to "Avoir"

"Avoir" (to have) is the second most important verb in French, and like "être," it's irregular. It's essential for:

  • Expressing possession
  • Using with many fixed expressions
  • Forming the compound past tense (passé composé)

Infinitive: Avoir (to have)

4.2 Present Tense Conjugation of "Avoir"

Subject French English Pronunciation
I j'ai I have zhay
You (informal) tu as You have too-ah
He / It il a He has eel-ah
She / It elle a She has el-ah
One / We (informal) on a One has / We have ohn-ah
We nous avons We have noo-ah-vohn
You (formal/plural) vous avez You have voo-zah-vay
They (masculine) ils ont They have eel-zohn
They (feminine) elles ont They have el-zohn

Complete Table:

Pronoun Conjugation
Je ai
Tu as
Il/Elle/On a
Nous avons
Vous avez
Ils/Elles ont

4.3 Using "Avoir" – Main Functions

1. Expressing Possession:

  • "J'ai un chat." (I have a cat.)
  • "Tu as une voiture?" (Do you have a car?)
  • "Il a deux enfants." (He has two children.)
  • "Elle a une maison." (She has a house.)
  • "Nous avons un chien." (We have a dog.)

2. Fixed Expressions (être + noun):

These are very important! French uses "avoir" where English uses "to be":

French English Literal
avoir faim to be hungry to have hunger
avoir soif to be thirsty to have thirst
avoir froid to be cold to have cold
avoir chaud to be hot to have hot
avoir peur (de) to be afraid (of) to have fear
avoir mal (à) to hurt / to have pain to have pain
avoir besoin (de) to need to have need
avoir envie (de) to want / to feel like to have desire
avoir sommeil to be sleepy to have sleep
avoir raison to be right to have reason
avoir tort to be wrong to have wrong

Examples:

  • "J'ai faim." (I'm hungry / I have hunger.)
  • "Tu as froid?" (Are you cold? / Do you have cold?)
  • "Il a peur des araignées." (He is afraid of spiders.)
  • "Elle a mal à la tête." (She has a headache / Her head hurts.)
  • "Nous avons besoin d'eau." (We need water.)
  • "Vous avez sommeil?" (Are you sleepy?)
  • "Ils ont raison." (They are right.)

4.4 Example Sentences with "Avoir"

Possessions:

  1. "J'ai un stylo." – I have a pen.
  2. "Tu as des livres?" – Do you have books?
  3. "Il a une soeur." – He has a sister.
  4. "Elle a un appartement." – She has an apartment.
  5. "Nous avons trois chats." – We have three cats.

UNIT 10: ARTICLES (DEFINITE & INDEFINITE)

10.1 Introduction to Articles

An article is a small word that comes before a noun. In English, we have "a," "an," and "the." French has more articles with different forms based on:

  • Gender (masculine, feminine)
  • Number (singular, plural)
  • Whether the noun starts with a vowel sound

10.2 Definite Articles (The)

Definite articles are used when talking about specific things you already know about.

Forms:

Gender & Number Form Used Before
Masculine singular le Consonant sound: le livre (the book)
Feminine singular la Consonant sound: la table (the table)
Singular (both genders) l' Vowel sound: l'ami (the friend), l'école (the school)
Plural (both genders) les Any noun: les livres, les tables, les amis

Examples:

French English
le chat the cat
la femme the woman
l'homme the man
l'école the school
les enfants the children
les arbres the trees

When to Use Definite Articles:

  1. Specific things you're talking about:

    • "J'ai le livre." (I have the book - you know which one)
    • "Où est le chat?" (Where is the cat? - you know which cat)

  2. Talking about things in general:

    • "Le français est une belle langue." (French is a beautiful language.)
    • "J'aime les chats." (I like cats - in general)

  3. Titles of books, movies, etc.:

    • "Le Seigneur des Anneaux" (The Lord of the Rings)
    • "La Reine Élisabeth" (Queen Elizabeth)

  4. Days of the week when you mean a regular occurrence:

    • "Le lundi, je vais à l'école." (On Mondays, I go to school.)
    • "Je travaille le samedi." (I work on Saturdays.)

10.3 Indefinite Articles (A/An, Some)

Indefinite articles are used for non-specific things or ones you're mentioning for the first time.

Forms:

Gender & Number Form Used Before
Masculine singular un Any noun: un livre (a book)
Feminine singular une Any noun: une table (a table)
Plural (both genders) des Any noun: des livres (some books/books)

Examples:

French English
un chat a cat
une femme a woman
des enfants some children / children
des arbres some trees / trees

When to Use Indefinite Articles:

  1. When something is new or unspecified:

    • "J'ai un chat." (I have a cat - you don't know which cat yet)
    • "Voilà une femme." (Here's a woman.)

  2. When you don't know which specific one:

    • "Je cherche un livre." (I'm looking for a book - any book)
    • "Tu as une sœur?" (Do you have a sister? - referring to any sister)

  3. Plural means "some" or just the plural:

    • "Je vois des oiseaux." (I see some birds / I see birds.)

10.4 Partitive Articles (Some)

The partitive article is used when you're talking about an uncount able amount of something (not a specific number).

Forms:

Gender Form Used Before
Masculine du Consonant: du pain (some bread)
Feminine de la Consonant: de la eau → BUT de l'eau
Before vowel/h de l' Both: de l'eau (some water), de l'argent (some money)
Plural des Both: des fruits (some fruits)

Partitive vs. Indefinite Plural:

  • "des" from indefinite: "J'ai des chats." – I have cats (countable - you can count: 1, 2, 3 cats)
  • "du" or "de la" (partitive): "Je veux du café." – I want some coffee (uncountable - you can't count individual coffee units)

Examples:

French English Type

An adjective is a word that describes a noun. In French, adjectives must agree with the noun they describe in:

  • Number (singular or plural)

This is different from English, where adjectives never change!

English vs. French:

In English, adjectives always come before the noun: "a big house."

  • Nationality/Origin: un homme français (a French man), une femme italienne (an Italian woman)
  • Size (usually): une maison grande (a big house)
  • Shape: une table ronde (a round table), une boîte carrée (a square box)
  • Material: une table en bois (a wooden table), un verre en cristal (a crystal glass)
  • Many descriptive adjectives: un livre intéressant (an interesting book), une personne intelligente (an intelligent person)

Adjectives That Come BEFORE the Noun (BANGS):

These common adjectives go before the noun. Use the acronym BANGS:

  • Beauty: beau (beautiful), joli (pretty), horrible, charmant (charming)
  • Age: jeune (young), vieux (old), ancien (old/former), nouveau (new)
  • Number: plusieurs (several), peu (few), quelques (some)
  • Goodness: bon (good), mauvais (bad), gentil (kind), méchant (mean)
  • Size: grand (big), petit (small), long (long), court (short)

Examples:

  • "un beau jardin" (a beautiful garden)
  • "une jeune femme" (a young woman)
  • "une petite maison" (a small house)
  • "un bon ami" (a good friend)
  • "une nouvelle voiture" (a new car)

  1. One BANGS + one non-BANGS: BANGS comes first

    • "une belle robe rouge" (a beautiful red dress)
    • "un jeune homme intelligent" (a young intelligent man)

  2. Two BANGS adjectives: Follow BANGS order, or alphabetical

    • "un jeune et beau garçon" (a young and beautiful boy)
    • "un petit nouveau jardin" (a small new garden)

  3. Two non-BANGS adjectives: Usually alphabetical, or separated by "et" (and)

    • "une personne intelligente et gentille" (an intelligent and kind person)
    • "une maison grande et rouge" (a big and red house)

UNIT 12: REGULAR VERBS (-ER, -IR, -RE)

12.1 Introduction to French Verbs

Verbs are action words. In French, verbs in the infinitive form (the "base" form) end in:

  • -ER (most common – about 80% of verbs)
  • -IR (many verbs)
  • -RE (fewer verbs)

When you conjugate a verb, you change its ending to match the subject:

  • Je parle (I speak)
  • Tu parles (You speak)
  • Il parle (He speaks)

13.1 Introduction to Reflexive Verbs

A reflexive verb is a verb where the action reflects back on the subject. In French, these verbs have "se" in the infinitive form:

  • se réveiller (to wake up [oneself])
  • se lever (to get up [oneself])
  • se coucher (to go to bed [oneself])

The reflexive pronoun changes based on the subject:

Subject Reflexive Pronoun Example
Je me je me lève
Tu te tu te lèves
Il/Elle/On se il/elle se lève
Nous nous nous nous levons
Vous vous vous vous levez
Ils/Elles se ils/elles se lèvent

Why Are They Called "Reflexive"?

The action goes "back" to the subject:

  • "Je me lave" = "I wash myself"
  • "Tu te prépares" = "You prepare yourself"
  • "Il se réveille" = "He wakes himself up"

18.1 Introduction

The passé composé is used to talk about actions that happened in the past. It's called "compound" because it uses TWO verbs:

Structure: Subject + auxiliary (avoir/être) + past participle

18.2 Past Participles

CONCLUSION & STUDY TIPS

Summary of A1 Level Competencies

At the A1 level, you should be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and provide basic personal information
  2. Greet people formally and informally
  3. Describe people (family, friends, colleagues)
  4. Talk about daily routines and habits
  5. Order food and talk about eating preferences
  6. Ask and answer simple questions
  7. Give and follow simple directions
  8. Talk about hobbies and what you like/dislike
  9. Use present, past, and future tenses in simple contexts
  10. Understand and produce basic French sentences in common situations

Study Recommendations

  1. Master the fundamentals first:

    • Pronunciation and the French sound system
    • The verbs "être" and "avoir"
    • Basic articles and gender
    • Regular verb conjugations

  2. Use spaced repetition:

    • Review vocabulary regularly
    • Practice conjugations multiple times
    • Return to earlier units frequently

  3. Listen actively:

    • Watch French movies and TV shows
    • Listen to French songs and podcasts
    • Pay attention to Quebec French pronunciation

  4. Practice speaking:

    • Find a language partner
    • Speak to yourself in French
    • Record yourself and listen back
    • Join French conversation groups

  5. Read regularly:

    • Start with simple children's books
    • Read French news articles
    • Read French blogs and social media posts
    • Use bilingual texts

  6. Write practice:

    • Keep a journal in French
    • Write emails to pen pals
    • Complete writing exercises
    • Describe your day in French

  7. Immerse in Quebec culture:

    • Watch Québécois TV shows and films
    • Listen to Quebec music
    • Follow Quebec social media accounts
    • Join Quebec community groups if possible

Canadian French Learning Resources

  • TV Québéc (Quebec television)
  • Radio-Canada (Canadian French broadcasting)
  • Quebec newspapers: La Presse, Le Devoir, The Globe and Mail (French edition)
  • Movies: Films from Quebec directors like Denis Villeneuve
  • Music: Artists from Quebec like Céline Dion, Leonard Cohen, etc.

Final Notes

Remember that learning French is a journey, not a race. The A1 level is just the beginning! Once you've mastered this level, you'll be ready for A2, where you'll expand your abilities significantly.

Keep practicing, stay motivated, and embrace the beautiful Quebec French language and culture!
